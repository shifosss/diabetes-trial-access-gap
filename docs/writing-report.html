The Geography of Disparity in Diabetes Clinical Trial Access

JSC370 Final Project — Deliverable Report

Author

Chen Zhang

Published

April 19, 2026

Full source and pipeline: github.com/shifosss/JSC370-Midterm-Proj. Interactive versions of selected figures are on the Interactive Visualizations page.

1 Introduction

1.1 Background and Motivation

Diabetes mellitus represents a major public health crisis in the United States, exhibiting profound geographic and socioeconomic disparities (Barker et al., 2011). Socioeconomic status, including factors such as poverty, education, and health insurance, plays a critical role in the prevalence and management of the disease (Hill-Briggs et al., 2021). As the medical industry continues to develop innovative treatments for Type 2 diabetes, a paradox remains: the opportunities to participate in clinical trials are not distributed equitably across the national geography. Clinical trial locations are frequently driven by institutional infrastructure and socioeconomic environments, creating a spatial mismatch that systematically excludes vulnerable, rural, and high-burden populations from research (Tanner et al., 2015; Unger et al., 2019). This lack of access often manifests in the form of physical travel barriers and a lack of local trial availability, directly limiting the generalizability of clinical research (Kirkwood et al., 2024).

1.2 Research Questions and Hypotheses

This project addresses two related but distinct aims:

  1. Descriptive aim: To what extent is the geographic distribution of U.S. Type 2 diabetes clinical trial sites aligned with the spatial pattern of disease burden? Where are the largest gaps between trial availability and local need?

  2. Modeling aim: Does including clinical trial access measures (local trial density and distance to the nearest trial site) improve prediction of county-level diabetes prevalence beyond a model that uses only socioeconomic and demographic characteristics, and which features carry the most predictive signal?

We hypothesize that (1) trial sites are spatially concentrated in socioeconomically advantaged counties with established research infrastructure rather than in the highest-burden areas, and (2) adding trial-access variables to an SES-only prediction model will yield little improvement in cross-validated accuracy, consistent with trial access being a redundant predictor once socioeconomic structure is already in the feature set.

2 Methods

2.1 Data Acquisition

The full dataset was assembled exclusively via programmatic API pulls to compile a multi-dimensional, county-level dataset of the United States. We obtained four categories of data, each pinned to a specific reference period:

  1. Clinical trial access — ClinicalTrials.gov v2 API: all U.S. Type 2 diabetes studies ever registered (no date filter), providing a cumulative snapshot of where trial infrastructure has been placed.
  2. Disease burden — CDC PLACES: county-level age-adjusted diabetes prevalence and related health measures, retaining the latest available year per county-measure pair (2022 release cycle, with underlying BRFSS data covering 2020–2022).
  3. Socioeconomic context — American Community Survey (ACS) 5-Year 2022 estimates (pooling survey responses from 2018–2022) for county and state covariates including poverty, income, education, insurance, and demographic composition.
  4. Healthcare infrastructure — NPI Registry: endocrinologist counts and academic medical center presence by county.

Supplementary contextual variables were drawn from the 2020 Census Decennial (rural population share by state) and a hardcoded lookup of Medicaid expansion status as of January 2024. The 2022 Census Gazetteer and FCC Census API were used for geocoding (see below).

All sources are cross-sectional. Because the ACS 5-Year estimates represent a rolling average centered roughly on 2020, the CDC PLACES burden estimates reflect a similar BRFSS window (2020–2022), and the cumulative trial registry captures long-run placement patterns, temporal misalignment across sources is modest. The unit of analysis — county structural characteristics and trial infrastructure — changes slowly relative to the one- to two-year offsets between data vintages.

2.2 Data Cleaning and Wrangling

The raw nested JSON payloads from ClinicalTrials.gov were flattened into separate trial-level and site-level records. Because the clinical trial data provided city/state locations rather than standard FIPS identifiers, we used the 2022 Census Gazetteer combined with the FCC Census API (reverse geocoding latitude/longitude to FIPS codes) to map trial sites to 5-digit County FIPS codes. To measure geographic isolation, we calculated the straight-line distance (using the Haversine formula and scipy.spatial.cKDTree) from each county’s population centroid to the nearest clinical trial site. All datasets were merged on the County FIPS code. Missing values for core modeling features were handled via complete-case analysis. Highly skewed variables (total population, trial distance, and trial density) were log-transformed (log1p or log) to stabilize variance.

2.3 Analytical Tools and Modeling

EDA and feature engineering used pandas and numpy. Visualizations were produced with seaborn and matplotlib. For the modeling aim, the response variable is county-level age-adjusted diabetes prevalence (places_diabetes from CDC PLACES). The 13 predictors fall into two groups: trial-access variables (log trial density, log distance to nearest site) and socioeconomic/demographic covariates (poverty rate, median household income, Gini index, uninsured rate, unemployment rate, bachelor’s degree attainment, vehicle access, internet access, % non-Hispanic Black, % Hispanic, and log population).

The modeling strategy has two components:

Baseline vs. augmented prediction comparison. To isolate the predictive contribution of trial-access features, we fit each model in two configurations: an SES-only baseline using the 11 socioeconomic/demographic predictors, and a full model adding the 2 trial-access predictors (13 total). Comparing cross-validated R², RMSE, and MAE between configurations quantifies the incremental lift from trial-access features. Three model families are used:

  • Elastic Net (scikit-learn ElasticNetCV, 5-fold CV over five l1_ratio values and 100 alpha values, predictors standardized): a regularized linear model whose coefficient magnitudes indicate feature importance under linearity.
  • Random Forest (scikit-learn RandomForestRegressor, 500 trees): a non-linear ensemble; permutation importance ranks features by their contribution to out-of-sample accuracy.
  • XGBoost (xgboost.XGBRegressor): a gradient-boosted tree ensemble that typically achieves the highest predictive accuracy among the three.

Feature importance analysis. SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values from the best-performing model decompose each prediction into per-feature contributions, enabling group-level comparison of trial-access vs. socioeconomic predictor importance. Permutation importance from Random Forest and coefficient magnitudes from Elastic Net provide complementary views under different modeling assumptions.

All models were evaluated under the same 5-fold cross-validation scheme (shuffled, random_state=42). Performance was compared on cross-validated R², RMSE, and MAE.

3 Preliminary Results

The data pipeline aggregated 3,646 U.S. Type 2 diabetes studies and 47,118 site records. The descriptive results presented below address Aim 1 at the state level, where every state has trial activity, and then extend to the county level to examine spatial access gaps. Because 73.4% of U.S. counties host no diabetes trial site at all, state-level summaries serve as the primary descriptive lens; county-level results supplement them by quantifying the within-state access variation that state aggregates mask.

3.1 State-Level Burden and Trial Density Do Not Line Up Cleanly

Figure 1 shows the central descriptive mismatch. States with heavier diabetes burden are not automatically the states with denser trial availability. The diabetes belt remains visible in the South and Appalachia, but the trial-density map is more fragmented and includes several smaller, infrastructure-rich states that do not rank among the highest-burden areas. This visual divergence motivates a more explicit comparison between observed and expected trial density.

To formalize that mismatch, I computed a state-level coverage residual: observed trial density minus the trial density expected for a state’s diabetes-burden decile. Negative values indicate under-coverage relative to peer states with similar burden, while positive values indicate over-coverage.

The residual analysis sharpens the map comparison. Several high-burden or moderately high-burden states appear under-covered relative to peers in the same burden decile, while some Mountain Plains and small-jurisdiction states appear over-covered. Sponsor composition is also concentrated: nationally, industry-sponsored trial density is 5.39 trials per 100k population versus 0.78 for non-industry trials, so industry density is about 6.9 times higher. This pattern is consistent with the possibility that site placement is influenced by sponsor logistics and institutional presence, though the residual analysis alone cannot confirm that mechanism.

3.2 County-Level Access Gaps Are Large

While state-level summaries provide a tractable overview, they can mask substantial within-state variation. The county file retains 3,221 counties. Only 856 counties (26.6%) host at least one diabetes trial site, while 2,365 counties (73.4%) have none. This sparsity is an important caveat: county-level trial density is zero-inflated, so county-level modeling (Aim 2) relies heavily on the distance-to-nearest-site measure rather than local trial counts to capture access variation.

That pattern matters for the research question because a state can look moderately well-covered in aggregate while still leaving most of its counties without feasible local trial access.

3.3 Endocrinologist Density and Trial Density Co-Occur

Among counties that host at least one trial site, those with higher endocrinologist density also tend to have higher trial density (Figure 4). This bivariate association is descriptive and does not establish a causal link; both variables may simply reflect the presence of academic medical centers or large health systems.

This co-occurrence is consistent with the hypothesis that trial placement tracks institutional infrastructure, but the bivariate scatter alone cannot distinguish infrastructure-driven placement from other explanations (e.g., population size driving both). The predictive models in the final analysis (Aim 2) will determine whether these features contribute independent predictive signal for county diabetes burden beyond what SES covariates already capture.

4 Summary

The descriptive results are consistent with the project hypothesis but do not yet confirm it. At the state level, trial density does not align smoothly with diabetes burden, and the coverage-residual analysis reveals systematic variation across burden deciles. At the county level, the majority of counties (73.4%) host no trial site, and travel distances for residents of those counties are often substantial. Endocrinologist density and trial density co-occur, but this bivariate pattern cannot isolate the role of infrastructure from confounding factors such as population size.

These findings are exploratory and descriptive. They identify suggestive patterns but cannot determine whether trial-access variables improve prediction of county-level diabetes prevalence (the response variable for Aim 2) beyond what socioeconomic and demographic features already capture.

For the final deliverable, the plan is:

  1. Baseline vs. augmented prediction comparison: Fit SES-only and SES + trial-access models (Elastic Net, Random Forest, XGBoost) under 5-fold cross-validation. Compare cross-validated R², RMSE, and MAE to quantify whether trial-access features improve out-of-sample prediction of county diabetes burden.
  2. Feature importance analysis: Use SHAP values and permutation importance from the best-performing model to rank which features carry the most predictive signal for county diabetes burden, and compare the aggregate importance of the trial-access group vs. the socioeconomic group.
  3. Interactive visualizations: Provide interactive maps and exploratory tools that allow users to examine the spatial relationships between burden, access, and infrastructure at both the state and county levels.
